PIK Income Hits 8.5% Universe Median. Is That a Signal — Or Just the New Normal?

When a borrower can't pay cash interest, many BDC credit agreements allow them to add that interest to the principal balance instead. This is called payment-in-kind, or PIK. The lender doesn't receive cash — they receive a larger loan. On paper, the income line grows. In reality, the collection date moves further into the future.

Three years ago, the median BDC in our universe had PIK income at roughly 5% of total investment income. Today it sits at 8.5%. That is a 70% increase. Twenty-one of 44 traded BDCs are above 8%. Four are above 15%.

The Question
Is rising PIK a sign of borrower stress — or has the middle market simply gotten better at negotiating non-cash payment flexibility into credit agreements as a standard tool?
PIK % Distribution Across 44 Traded BDCs
PIK % of Total Investment IncomeQ3-Q4 2025 · 44 traded BDCs

There are two schools of thought. The stress camp argues that PIK income is a leading indicator of borrower distress — companies that can't pay cash interest today are more likely to default tomorrow. If the PIK loan eventually becomes non-accrual, all that accrued income gets reversed in a single quarter.

The structural camp argues that PIK has become a negotiating tool, not a distress signal. Borrowers in strong positions can demand PIK flexibility as part of the credit terms — essentially a free option to defer cash payments when capital allocation is better used elsewhere. On this view, rising PIK might actually reflect strong demand for private credit from high-quality borrowers.

The data doesn't cleanly resolve this debate. But it does show that the funds with the highest PIK ratios also tend to have the highest non-accrual rates — suggesting the two are correlated, even if causality is unclear. Our EQS scoring reflects this by treating high PIK as a negative signal in the D3 dimension.

The honest answer is: we don't know yet. PIK is manageable when credit quality is stable and rates are high — the accrued interest keeps compounding. It becomes problematic when credit quality deteriorates and the borrower can't refinance. We'll be watching non-accrual trends at high-PIK funds closely over the next two quarters.
