Reading the Data.

A non accrual percentage of 2.3 is either reassuring or alarming. The number alone tells you nothing. You need the distribution. This primer shows where every BDC sits on every metric, computed live from the platform, so you know in seconds whether any reading is high, normal, or worrying.

9 min read Live data from the platform Updated every page load By Qazi Rasool

01Why a number without a distribution is useless.

If someone tells you "the non accrual rate at fund X is 2.3 percent," what do you do with that? On its own, nothing. You cannot tell whether 2.3 is high or low until you know what the rest of the universe looks like. Maybe the median BDC sits at 1.8 percent and 2.3 is mild stress. Maybe the median is at 4.2 and 2.3 is excellent.

This is the problem this primer exists to solve. Every metric on this platform is shown below alongside its actual distribution across the 134 BDCs we cover, recomputed every time you load the page. You will see the median, the 25th percentile, the 75th percentile, and the practical bands that an experienced credit analyst would read each metric against.

The colored bands work like this:

Healthy band. Where you want a fund to sit. Watch band. Worth a closer look but not alarming on its own. Risk band. The fund is doing something different from peers. Could be skill, could be stress.

"Healthy" and "Risk" are not value judgments. A fund in the risk band is not necessarily in distress; it is just an outlier worth understanding. Higher leverage might be the manager's strategy. Lower yield might mean they are competing on quality. The point is to know.

02The metrics, with their live distributions.

What follows is every commonly used BDC metric, shown with its real distribution across the universe. Numbers below the bars are the 25th percentile, median, and 75th percentile, computed live from the latest quarterly data. Live

NAV per share
Higher generally better
Net asset value divided by shares outstanding. The simplest economic ownership measure. A rising NAV/share means the fund is creating value per dollar invested. A falling one means losses, dividend overpayment, or both.
Computing distribution from universe...
How to read it. The absolute number is less meaningful than the trend. A BDC trading at $20/share NAV is fine. A BDC whose NAV has fallen from $25 to $20 over four quarters is in trouble. Always compare NAV per share to its history before its peers.
Non accrual % (on cost)
Lower is better
The percentage of the loan book (by cost basis) where the borrower has stopped paying interest. The single most important credit indicator on a BDC balance sheet. Rising non accruals are the canonical signal of deteriorating portfolio health.
Computing distribution from universe...
How to read it. Anything under 2 percent is healthy. 2 to 4 percent is normal mid cycle. Above 4 percent suggests real stress in the book. Above 6 percent is a warning. Always cross check the "on cost" reading with "on fair value" because the gap between the two tells you how much the market has already marked down.
PIK %
Lower is better
Percentage of income paid in kind rather than cash. PIK lets a borrower defer interest by issuing more debt. Common in mezz and some second lien deals by design. Rising PIK in a fund's overall book is often a leading indicator that borrowers are conserving cash.
Computing distribution from universe...
How to read it. Some PIK exposure is fine and expected, especially in mezz heavy funds. Under 5 percent is comfortable. 5 to 12 percent is a fund that has meaningful mezz or has been quietly accepting PIK toggles. Above 12 percent suggests the manager has been allowing borrowers to defer cash interest at material scale.
Weighted average yield
Context matters
Income generating yield of the loan portfolio. Reflects the all in coupon (base rate plus spread) the fund is collecting on its debt investments, weighted by position size.
Computing distribution from universe...
How to read it. The current SOFR base rate puts most BDC weighted yields between 10 and 13 percent. A fund above the high end of the range is reaching for yield, either via riskier credit or via subordinated tranches. A fund well below is either holding a lot of cash, has heavy first lien exposure to large investment grade adjacent borrowers, or has just refinanced into tighter spreads.
Debt to equity ratio
Strategy dependent
Total debt outstanding divided by net assets. The standard leverage measure for a BDC. Regulated cap is 2.0x (since the 2018 Small Business Credit Availability Act). Most large BDCs operate between 0.9x and 1.3x.
Computing distribution from universe...
How to read it. Below 0.7x is conservative, may indicate dry powder or risk aversion. 0.9 to 1.3x is the operating range for most large BDCs. 1.4x to 1.8x is aggressive but within the regulated framework. Above 1.8x means the fund is close to its statutory cap. Leverage approaching the cap is a forced selling risk if the portfolio marks down.
First lien %
Higher is safer
Share of the portfolio (at fair value) in first lien senior secured loans. The top of the capital structure. First lien debt has first claim on borrower collateral in default and historically recovers 70 to 90 cents on the dollar.
Computing distribution from universe...
How to read it. Above 80 percent is a senior secured focused strategy, the conservative end of BDC investing. 60 to 80 percent is the typical broad based BDC. Below 50 percent indicates a fund with meaningful exposure to second lien, mezz, or equity. Lower first lien is not automatically bad; it indicates higher target return and higher risk.

03How to use this in practice.

Three habits worth building, in order:

First, never read a number in isolation. Always check where it sits in the distribution. The platform shows this everywhere. Use it.

Second, watch the trend more than the level. A fund whose non accrual % has moved from 1.5 to 3.5 over four quarters is more concerning than a fund stable at 4.0. Direction beats magnitude in credit work.

Third, when something is in the risk band, find out why. Outliers exist for reasons. Sometimes the reason is skill (a unitranche specialist will have higher yields than a senior focused fund). Sometimes the reason is stress. The job is to know which.

Distributions are how a credit analyst stops reading numbers as facts and starts reading them as positions.
