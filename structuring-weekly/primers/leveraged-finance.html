Leveraged Finance, Mechanics and Vocabulary.

The plumbing of how a leveraged buyout actually gets financed. Term loan B markets, high yield bonds, spreads and OID, call protection, covenants. Read this and the next time someone says "TLB priced at SOFR plus 425 with a 99 OID and 101 soft call for six months" you will hear an actual sentence.

16 min read 6 interactives Updated 2026 By Qazi Rasool

01What leveraged finance is.

Leveraged finance is the business of lending money to companies with significant existing debt, typically used to fund leveraged buyouts, recapitalizations, refinancings, and acquisitions. The word "leveraged" is the operative one. These borrowers run with total debt to EBITDA ratios of four to seven times. They are rated below investment grade, usually BB or B. They pay a meaningful premium over investment grade credit. They are, in short, the engine of private equity.

The leveraged finance market splits into three main instrument types. Leveraged loans (syndicated and private), high yield bonds, and mezzanine debt. Each occupies a different rung in the capital structure with different economics, covenants, and investor bases.

02The TLB and how it gets priced.

The term loan B (TLB) is the dominant leveraged loan instrument in the US market. It is senior secured, floating rate, and syndicated to a broad investor base of CLOs, mutual funds, and prime brokerage clients. Pricing has three components, and the math is more subtle than it looks.

Three things to notice. OID amortizes into the yield over the life of the loan. A 100 basis point OID adds about 20 bps per year to the yield on a 5 year deal, more on a shorter one. OID is the market's stress signal. When demand is strong, TLBs price at par or even at a 99.5 OID. When demand is weak, you see 97 or 98 prints. The OID is the cleanest read on syndicate appetite. The cash coupon and the yield are different things. Quoting "SOFR plus 425" tells you the cash coupon. Quoting "SOFR plus 425, 99 OID, 5 year" tells you the yield. Lenders care about the yield.

03Covenants. The thing that has nearly disappeared.

Covenants are contractual promises a borrower makes to its lenders. Two kinds matter: maintenance covenants require the borrower to maintain certain financial ratios (typically a max leverage ratio, a min interest coverage ratio) tested every quarter. Incurrence covenants only get tested when the borrower wants to do something specific (issue more debt, make a restricted payment, pay a dividend).

The leveraged loan market over the last fifteen years has steadily eroded covenants. The endpoint of that erosion is the cov lite loan: no maintenance covenants at all, just incurrence covenants. Today over 90 percent of syndicated TLBs are cov lite. Direct lending is the meaningful exception. Most direct loans still have full maintenance covenants because the lender is sitting across the table and can demand them.

The disappearance of maintenance covenants in the syndicated market is the single biggest structural shift in leveraged finance over the last fifteen years. It is also one of the main reasons private credit has grown so fast. When borrowers find that maintenance covenants are no longer available in the syndicated market, the ones who actually want or accept them go to direct lenders, who happen to pay 200 to 400 bps more for the same credit risk in exchange.

04Call protection.

Call protection prevents the borrower from refinancing the loan too quickly. It exists because lenders priced the loan assuming a certain hold period; if the borrower refinances out at par six months later, the lender's actual yield was a small fraction of the underwriting yield. Call protection is the lender's compensation for taking that risk.

05The capital stack in an LBO.

When a sponsor buys a company in a leveraged buyout, the financing is built in layers. Senior loans at the top with first claim on collateral, layered tranches of mezzanine and subordinated debt below, sponsor equity at the bottom. Each layer pays a higher coupon than the one above because it takes more risk.

Two things to internalize from this view. Senior loans cap out at around 4.5x EBITDA; senior lenders will not go higher because at that point recovery in default starts to drop materially. Anything beyond 4.5x has to be raised in junior tranches that pay 200 to 500 bps more. Equity is the residual. Sponsors do not pick the equity amount; it falls out of the math (enterprise value minus total debt equals equity).

06How TLBs trade in the secondary.

Once a TLB is closed and the loan is syndicated, the loan trades. Not heavily; the loan market is meaningfully less liquid than the bond market. But trades happen, marks move, and price action matters. A loan that closes at par and trades down to 92 cents tells you something. A loan that gets stuck at 95 in a healthy market is being treated as a credit concern.

The yield to maturity rises faster than the current yield as price drops because the investor also gains the pull to par at maturity. A loan at 80 with a 9 percent coupon has an 11 percent current yield but a 14 to 15 percent yield to maturity. When you see funds buying mid 80s paper in the secondary, they are betting on the pull to par. They lose if the loan defaults before then.

07Putting it together. The blended yield on a portfolio.

Most credit funds hold dozens to hundreds of positions at different yields, coupons, and prices. The blended yield is the size weighted average. Useful when comparing two portfolios, useful when answering "what does the fund actually earn." Build one below.

08Words you can now translate.

If you read this primer carefully you can now parse the following without help:

"The deal is a $750M TLB at SOFR plus 450, 99.5 OID, 7 year tenor, 101 soft call for 6 months, cov lite. Pricing tightened 25 bps from initial talk."

Translation. A 750 million dollar senior secured term loan, paying interest at SOFR (currently around 5 percent) plus 450 basis points, sold to investors at 99.5 cents on the dollar (50 bps of discount), maturing in 7 years, with a 1 percent call protection penalty that expires after 6 months. No financial maintenance covenants. The deal launched at SOFR plus 475 and the underwriters were able to bring it 25 bps tighter due to investor demand. Total yield to a buy and hold investor: roughly 9.6 percent at issue.

That is what leveraged finance vocabulary unlocks. The next primer covers what happens when the credit goes wrong.
