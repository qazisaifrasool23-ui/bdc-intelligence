The Business Development Company.

A type of investment fund Congress invented in 1980, almost forgotten for thirty years, then resurrected in the 2010s as the canonical vehicle for private credit. This is what a BDC actually is, how it differs from a closed end fund or a mutual fund, and why it is structured the way it is.

11 min read 4 interactives Updated 2026 By Qazi Rasool

01What a BDC is.

A business development company is a publicly registered closed end investment company created by the Small Business Investment Incentive Act of 1980. Its purpose, in plain terms, was to channel public market capital into smaller US private companies that struggled to access traditional debt markets. Congress wanted a vehicle that retail investors could buy through a regular brokerage account, but that could lend to private middle market borrowers the way a bank or a PE fund does.

The structure is governed by the Investment Company Act of 1940 (the same act that governs mutual funds and closed end funds), but with a specific BDC carve out that imposes its own rules. A BDC must invest at least 70% of its assets in "eligible portfolio companies", which broadly means private US operating companies. The remaining 30% can be cash, public debt, or other less restricted investments. This 70% test is the line that separates a BDC from a CLO equity fund or a syndicated loan fund.

The two structures serve very different purposes. The traded BDC is built to give a public market investor access to private credit returns alongside daily liquidity. The non traded BDC is built to deliver private credit exposure to a wealth channel client who values NAV stability over daily mark to market. Both have legitimate audiences. Both have structural risks the other does not.

04The tax structure that explains everything.

If you read a BDC quarterly and wondered why they distribute almost everything they earn, the answer is one word: RIC. Most BDCs elect to be taxed as a Regulated Investment Company under Subchapter M of the Internal Revenue Code. The election is voluntary, but the benefit is enormous: a RIC pays no corporate income tax on income or capital gains that it distributes to shareholders. To qualify, the fund has to distribute at least 90% of its taxable income each year.

This single rule shapes everything about how a BDC operates. It is why distributions look enormous (10 percent or more dividend yields are common). It is why retained earnings are essentially zero. It is why a BDC cannot grow by reinvesting earnings the way a normal corporation does. It is why a BDC has to raise external equity capital every time it wants to grow.

The thing to understand: the RIC tax shield is a regulatory feature that exists in service of the BDC's stated purpose (channeling capital to small US private companies). Congress effectively said: if you keep at least 70% of your assets invested in eligible portfolio companies AND distribute at least 90% of your income, we will not double tax you. Without that bargain, the entire BDC asset class would not exist in its current form.

BDCs are almost always externally managed, meaning a separate investment adviser (the manager) runs the portfolio in exchange for a management fee and an incentive fee. The structure varies, but the typical economics look like this. Adjust the inputs to see how the fees compound on a $5B fund.

Two things to notice. First, the base management fee is charged on total assets, not net assets, which means the manager earns the fee on the levered portion of the portfolio. Higher leverage equals more fee, which is why critics call this an incentive to over lever. Second, the incentive fee in most BDCs has a hurdle rate (usually 7 percent annually) and then a catch up provision such that the manager earns nothing below the hurdle and the full 20 percent of incremental income above it. The catch up is what turns a small move past 7 percent into a large fee.

06What to actually look at.

When you open a BDC quarterly report, the most useful metrics to read first, in order, are:

  • Net asset value per share, sequential change. Did it go up or down quarter over quarter? Most of the answer to "how is this fund doing" is in this single number.
  • Non accrual percentage on cost AND on fair value. Cost shows you what fraction of the loan book has stopped paying. Fair value shows you how much economic value the market thinks is at risk.
  • NII coverage of the dividend. A coverage ratio below 1.0 means the fund is paying out more than it earned. Sustained sub 1.0 coverage is the leading indicator of a dividend cut.
  • Investment activity, new originations versus repayments. Tells you whether the manager is deploying capital or shrinking. Net deployment in a tough cycle is a sign of confidence.
  • PIK income as a percentage of total income. PIK is "paid in kind", meaning the borrower pays interest in more debt rather than cash. Rising PIK percentages mean the underlying borrowers are conserving cash.
If you only learn one thing about BDCs, learn this: they are designed to give you an undiluted look at the underlying loan book every quarter, in detail, with line item disclosure. Almost no other asset class offers this level of transparency. The whole point of BDC Intelligence is to make that transparency usable.
