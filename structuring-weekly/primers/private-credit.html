Private Credit, From the Ground Up.

A market that did not exist at meaningful scale in 2010 now allocates roughly two trillion dollars of debt to companies the public markets never see. This is what it actually is, how it works, and why it kept growing.

14 min read 5 interactives Updated 2026 By Qazi Rasool

01What private credit actually is.

Private credit is a sentence away from being misunderstood. Most introductions describe it as "loans made by non bank lenders to private companies." That sentence is correct and almost useless. It tells you nothing about why the market exists, who is lending, what is being financed, or how the deals are structured. Let us replace it with something more honest.

Private credit is a debt market that operates outside the public bond and syndicated loan systems, in which a single lender or a small club of lenders negotiates a loan directly with a borrower, holds the position to maturity, and does not ordinarily trade it. The "private" does not mean the borrower is a private company. It means the loan itself is privately placed and privately held.

This matters because most of the differences from public credit follow from that one fact. If your loan does not trade, you do not need a credit rating to clear the trade. If you do not need a rating, you do not have to fit your borrower into Moody's and S&P's templates. If you negotiate the deal directly with the company, you can put covenants in that the syndicated market would never accept. If you hold to maturity, you are paid for taking illiquidity, and the premium is real.

03Where it came from.

Private credit did not appear out of nowhere. The cleanest explanation is that it filled a vacuum left when banks retreated from middle market lending after 2008. Basel III and Dodd Frank made capital expensive against held loans. Banks responded by either selling loans into the syndicated market or, for borrowers too small to syndicate, declining the deal entirely. The middle market did not stop wanting debt. The borrowers were still there. Someone had to lend.

The first wave of capital came from business development companies and a handful of asset managers (Ares, Apollo, Blackstone, KKR, Oaktree) that had been doing mezzanine lending for years and saw an opportunity to move up the capital structure into senior secured loans. Insurance companies, pension funds, and sovereign wealth funds followed as the asset class proved itself.

By 2018, direct lending had become a recognized asset allocation bucket. By 2023, it was the largest pool of dry powder in the entire alternatives industry. That is a fifteen year arc from "what is this" to "the largest line item in our alternatives book."

04Private credit versus syndicated loans.

The cleanest way to understand private credit is to compare it side by side against its public cousin, the syndicated loan market. Both finance the same companies (sponsor backed, leveraged, US middle market). Both are senior secured. Both are floating rate. But almost everything else is different.

Three differences end up mattering more than the others. Negotiation: in a syndicated deal you take what is on the term sheet because the deal has fifty other lenders agreeing. In direct lending you sit across the table and shape the terms. Covenants: syndicated loans have lost most of their covenants over the last decade; direct loans still have full maintenance covenants. Pricing: direct lending pays you 200 to 400 basis points over the equivalent syndicated loan, in exchange for accepting the illiquidity.

Notice what happens as you push leverage higher. The senior pieces stay roughly constant in size relative to EBITDA (a senior direct lender is rarely willing to go above 4.5× to 5.5× attach), so each additional turn of leverage piles into the riskier, more expensive tranches. By 7× the equity is a thin slice and the sub and mezz are doing most of the work. That is exactly how the 2021 and 2022 vintage deals were structured, and exactly why they have generated most of the non accruals in the current cycle.

07The risks worth knowing.

Private credit's pitch is straightforward: senior secured, floating rate, contractual coupon, illiquidity premium, low correlation to public markets. Most of that is true. But the asset class has real risks that the marketing materials soft pedal. Three deserve emphasis.

Mark to model, not market. Private loans do not trade. Funds value them quarterly using internal models and third party agents. When the cycle turns, the marks move slowly and ambiguously. By the time a position is marked to seventy, the underlying credit has often been deteriorating for two to four quarters. The reported NAV lags the economic reality.

Concentration. The same fifteen to twenty large borrowers show up across most large BDC and direct lending fund portfolios. When one of them defaults, multiple lenders are affected at once. The "low correlation" claim is genuine across sectors and macro factors; it is much weaker across specific borrowers.

Fund structure risk. Most private credit funds are open ended or perpetually offered. Investors can redeem on quarterly or monthly cycles. In a stressed market, redemptions can force the fund to either gate (locking investors in) or sell positions into a market with no real bid. This is the structural risk that everyone in the asset class quietly worries about and almost nobody talks about openly.

08What comes next.

You now have the shape of the market: what it is, where it came from, how it differs from public credit, how the strategies ladder, how a deal gets built, and where the real risks sit. The natural next step is to understand the structures that hold it: business development companies, the publicly visible window into roughly 250 billion dollars of private credit AUM. That is what the next primer covers.

If you remember one thing from this primer, remember that private credit is a market defined by what it is not. Not traded. Not rated. Not standardized. Almost everything that makes the asset class interesting flows from those three absences.
