Distressed Debt, From Stress to Emergence.

What happens when a leveraged borrower stops being able to pay its debt. The vocabulary of the workout. The waterfall in default. DIP financing and drop down transactions. The playbooks distressed funds run, and why the 2026 cycle looks different from 2008 or 2020.

18 min read 5 interactives Updated 2026 By Qazi Rasool

01What "distressed" actually means.

Distressed debt is debt of a company that the market believes will not be able to repay it on the original terms. The signal is in the price. A loan trading at 80 cents on the dollar is stressed. A loan at 60 is distressed. A loan at 30 is generally trading on recovery, meaning the market expects the company will restructure and the holder will receive some combination of cash, new debt, and equity in the reorganized entity worth roughly 30 cents.

The conventional academic definition is any debt yielding more than 1,000 basis points above the equivalent Treasury. In practice, distressed funds use a price threshold (typically below 80 cents for loans, below 70 cents for bonds) plus their own judgment about the situation. Some companies have stressed paper without ever defaulting. Some default within months of trading at par.

02From price to implied recovery.

The most useful single calculation in distressed work is the relationship between secondary price, the assumed time to a restructuring event, and the implied recovery on the debt. If you can buy a loan at 50 cents and recover 75 cents in 18 months, you have made 50 percent gross before discounting for the time and the risk. Adjust the inputs below to see the math.

Two truths fall out. Time kills returns. The same gross return spread over 36 months instead of 18 produces a dramatically lower IRR. The recovery assumption is the entire deal. Distressed investors spend most of their time understanding what the company is actually worth as a going concern, what the assets are worth in a liquidation, and which tranche they sit in. Everything else is detail.

03The priority waterfall.

When a company defaults and enters restructuring, the rules of the road are clear. Senior debt is paid first. Subordinated debt is paid next. Equity is paid last, often nothing. This is the priority waterfall, also called absolute priority rule. It is one of the most fundamental principles of bankruptcy law and the reason senior secured debt sits where it sits in the capital structure.

Interactive 02 of 05

The priority waterfall, three scenarios.

How to read it. Senior secured debt gets paid first up to its face value. If anything is left, second lien is next. Then sub debt. Whatever is left, equity. In a deep distress scenario where the company is worth less than the senior debt face, second lien and below recover nothing.

Two caveats. First, the priority waterfall applies before the restructuring plays out. Inside a Chapter 11, the waterfall is the starting point for negotiation, not the ending point. Junior creditors regularly negotiate small distributions ("tip") in exchange for not objecting to the plan. Second, modern indentures have created mechanisms by which senior debt can be primed by even more senior debt (drop down, uptier, J Crew, Serta) that effectively rewrite the priority waterfall mid restructuring. We will come back to this.

05DIP financing.

Debtor in possession financing is loans extended to a company that has filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy. The new lender provides operating capital so the business can continue while it restructures. In exchange, DIP lenders get extraordinary protections: super priority status ahead of all pre petition debt, liens on assets not previously pledged, and aggressive covenants that effectively let the DIP lender drive the restructuring.

Why this matters for BDCs. When a BDC borrower files for Chapter 11, the BDC's existing first lien position may be subordinated to a new DIP lender, who is often an outsider with no prior relationship with the borrower. Some larger BDCs (ARCC, OBDC, others) participate in their own borrowers' DIPs to maintain influence over the restructuring. This is one of the ways funds protect existing positions in default scenarios.

06The thing that broke the priority waterfall.

Over the last decade, distressed practitioners have invented and re invented mechanisms by which existing senior lenders can be effectively primed by other senior lenders, without those lenders' consent. The most common modern forms are drop down transactions (J Crew, Travelport) and uptier exchanges (Serta Simmons). Each works by exploiting loopholes in indenture language that the syndicated market has historically accepted as standard.

This is the structural reason credit analysts in 2026 do not assume their first lien position is actually first. Every modern indenture review starts with checking for unrestricted subsidiary baskets, builder baskets, and other escape hatches that allow the sponsor to engineer outcomes that put new debt ahead of existing senior secured.

07Distressed in 2026 looks different.

The defining feature of the current distressed cycle is that it is mostly private. Defaults are happening in BDC portfolios, in direct lending funds, in private credit warehouses. The names are unfamiliar because the deals were never syndicated. The recovery rates are harder to estimate because there are no public secondary marks. The restructurings are happening out of court, in private workouts negotiated bilaterally between the direct lender and the sponsor.

What does this mean in practice?

  • Marks lag reality more than they did in 2020. Public defaults price in days. Private credit marks move quarterly. The gap between "we know this is going to fail" and "the fund has marked it" can be 6 to 12 months.
  • Recovery rates are bifurcating. Direct lending deals with full maintenance covenants and tight controls recover well. Cov lite syndicated paper from 2020 to 2022 vintages recovers poorly.
  • The data is harder to get. No 8 K filings on private workouts. No Reorg coverage on a private credit restructuring. The only signal often is the BDC's own SOI showing the position marked down quarter after quarter.

This is precisely the gap BDC Intelligence is designed to close. Watching marks across the universe at the line item level surfaces patterns that no other public data source captures. A borrower marked from 95 to 80 to 70 across three quarters by three different lenders is a real signal even when no public news has been printed.

Distressed work in private credit is about reading the marks the way a doctor reads vital signs. Each individual reading is ambiguous. The trend is the diagnosis.
